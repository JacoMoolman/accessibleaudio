Purpose

Make more books accessible to more readers.

Accessible Audio is built for publishers, schools, libraries, and education teams that need printed catalogues to reach blind and visually impaired readers, multilingual learners, and communities underserved by conventional audiobook production. Manuscript text is processed locally for audiobook generation, rather than submitted to commercial cloud AI platforms.

The access gap

Publishing a book does not automatically make it accessible.

Around 2.2 to 2.4 million new books are estimated to be published worldwide each year, but only a small fraction become audiobooks. Available industry data suggests audiobook releases represent at least 3-5% of annual book production, with the majority of books still never reaching listeners.

In South Africa, the gap is sharper. Roughly 8,500 to 10,000 books are recorded annually depending on counting method, while audiobook output remains small and poorly measured. Indigenous-language educational publishing has made progress, but book-to-audio, translation, and braille access are still not routine parts of the publishing pipeline.

Why audio access is hard to scale

Traditional audiobook production is expensive.

Professional audiobook quotes are usually built around a finished hour: one hour of final audio after narration, editing, proofing, mastering, pickups, and file export. A normal 70,000 to 90,000-word book can easily become an 8 to 10 hour audiobook.

A professionally produced audiobook in South Africa often falls between R6,000 and R9,000 per finished hour, with full studio production models rising higher. That puts many standard-length books in a R60,000-R85,000 planning range before publishers add translation, multiple language editions, or broader accessibility.

Private production

Audiobook generation is handled locally.

The production process uses open-source AI models and custom fine-tuned voice models running on local systems. For audiobook generation, submitted book text is not sent to commercial cloud AI services or large provider training pipelines.

This gives publishers a clearer privacy position when testing a title, chapter sample, or sensitive education catalogue. Distribution and streaming support can be scoped separately after production.
